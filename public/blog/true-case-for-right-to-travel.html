The Real Case for the Right to Drive Without a License

Published July 24, 2026 • 10 min read

The government tells you driving is a "privilege." They say the roads are regulated by the state. They say you need their permission to move freely in your own country.

But what if they're wrong?

What if the Constitution — and 200 years of case law — actually supports your right to travel in the ordinary conveyance of the modern era without begging the government for a license?

Let's look at what the courts have actually said.

The Right to Travel Is Fundamental

The U.S. Supreme Court has repeatedly held that the right to travel is a fundamental constitutional right. It's not a privilege. It's not a perk. It's a right — protected by Article IV, the 14th Amendment, and the structure of the Constitution itself.

"The right to travel is a fundamental constitutional right... firmly embedded in our jurisprudence." Shapiro v. Thompson, 394 U.S. 618 (1969) — U.S. Supreme Court
"The right of a citizen to travel upon the public highways... is not a mere privilege which may be permitted or prohibited at will, but a common right which he has under his right to life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness." Thompson v. Smith, 154 SE 579 (Va. 1930)

The Supreme Court Has Defined Three Separate Components

In Saenz v. Roe (1999), the Supreme Court identified three aspects of the right to travel:

  1. The right to enter one state and leave another — protected by the Constitution from the Articles of Confederation onward
  2. The right to be treated as a welcome visitor — protected by Article IV's Privileges and Immunities Clause
  3. The right to be treated equally as a new resident — protected by the 14th Amendment

The Right Includes Ordinary Conveyances

Courts have consistently held that the right to travel includes the right to choose your mode of transportation. The automobile is the ordinary conveyance of our era.

"Each citizen has the absolute right to choose for himself the mode of conveyance he desires." Swift v. City of Topeka, 43 Kan. 671 (1890)
"The RIGHT to use an automobile on the public highways partakes of the nature of a liberty within the meaning of the Constitutional guarantees." Berberian v. Lussier, 139 A.2d 869 (R.I. 1958)
"The RIGHT of the citizen to DRIVE on the public street with freedom from police interference... is a FUNDAMENTAL CONSTITUTIONAL RIGHT which must be protected by the courts." People v. Horton, 14 Cal.App.3d 667 (1971)
"The right to operate a motor vehicle upon the public streets and highways is not a mere privilege. It is a right of liberty, the enjoyment of which is protected by the guarantees of the federal and state constitutions." Adams v. City of Pocatello, 416 P.2d 46 (Idaho 1966)

The Federal Government Agrees: "Motor Vehicle" Means Commercial

Under federal law — 18 U.S.C. § 31 — the definition of "motor vehicle" explicitly requires that the vehicle be "used for commercial purposes." A private citizen using their personal vehicle for personal travel does not fit this definition.

18 U.S.C. § 31(6): "The term 'motor vehicle' means every description of carriage or other contrivance propelled or drawn by mechanical power and used for commercial purposes on the highways..."
18 U.S.C. § 31(10): "The term 'used for commercial purposes' means the carriage of persons or property for any fare, fee, rate, charge or other consideration, or directly or indirectly in connection with any business, or other undertaking intended for profit."

State codes define "motor vehicle" more broadly, but the federal government recognizes the distinction between commercial and personal use. The question is whether state codes are overreaching.

The "Operator" Distinction

"The court makes it clear that a license relates to qualifications to engage in profession, business, trade or calling; thus, when merely traveling without compensation or profit... no license is required." Wingfield v. Fielder, 2d Ca. 3d 213 (Cal. App. 1972)

You Can Ignore an Unconstitutional Licensing Law

The U.S. Supreme Court has held that when a licensing scheme is unconstitutional, citizens may ignore it.

"Persons faced with an unconstitutional licensing law which purports to require a license as a prerequisite to exercise of right... may ignore the law and engage with impunity in exercise of such right." Shuttlesworth v. Birmingham, 394 U.S. 147 (1969) — U.S. Supreme Court
"If [state] officials construe a vague statute unconstitutionally, the citizen may take them at their word, and act on the assumption that the statute is void." Shuttlesworth v. Birmingham, 394 U.S. 147 (1969)

The State DMV - Corporate Entity Argument

"Those who have the right to do something cannot be licensed for what they already have right to do as such license would be meaningless." City of Chicago v. Collins, 51 NE 907 (Ill. 1898)
"A license means leave to do a thing which the licensor could prevent." Blatz Brewing Co. v. Collins, 160 P.2d 37 (Cal. 1945)

If the right to travel is a constitutional right, then a "license to travel" is either redundant (you already have the right) or a restriction (you need permission). Neither is consistent with the concept of an unalienable right.

What This Means

The argument that you don't need a driver's license rests on solid constitutional ground:

  1. The right to travel is a fundamental constitutional right
  2. It includes the right to choose ordinary conveyances — including the automobile
  3. Federal law defines "motor vehicle" as commercial, not personal
  4. Multiple courts have held that driving involves a liberty interest
  5. The Supreme Court has said unconstitutional licensing schemes can be ignored
  6. A "license" for something you already have a right to do is legally meaningless
