The We Are Change "No License" Article — Debunked Case by Case

Published July 24, 2026 • By the Right to Travel Legal Team • 8 min read

The single most circulated document in the "no license needed" community is an article published on WeAreChange.org titled "U.S. Supreme Court Says No License Necessary To Drive Automobile On Public Highways/Streets." It's been shared tens of thousands of times. It's cited in YouTube comments, sovereign citizen forums, and even traffic court filings.

It's also deeply misleading — not because the quotes are fake, but because they're taken out of context in ways that change their meaning entirely.

This article breaks down every major case cited, shows you the actual holding, and explains why none of them say what the article implies.

Why This Matters

If you're going to cite case law — in a traffic stop, in court, or in conversation — you need to know what the cases actually say. Misquoting precedent doesn't just lose arguments. It destroys credibility. A judge who catches you citing a case for something it doesn't hold will dismiss everything else you say.

We believe in the right to travel. That's exactly why we're doing this — because the argument is stronger when it's accurate.

The Core Problem in One Sentence

The article confuses two completely different legal questions:

Question 1: Do you have the right to use public highways?

Yes. Every case cited in the article establishes this correctly. The right to travel is real.

Question 2: Do you need a license to operate a motor vehicle on them?

Also yes — per Hendrick v. Maryland (1915) and every state statute. The article's cases don't address this question. They establish #1 but are presented as if they answer #2.

The Most-Cited Case: Thompson v. Smith

"The right of a citizen to travel upon the public highways and to transport his property thereon, by horsedrawn carriage, wagon, or automobile, is not a mere privilege which may be permitted or prohibited at will, but a common right." Thompson v. Smith, 154 SE 579 (Va. 1930)

This is the article's flagship quote. It sounds damning. But here's what the article doesn't tell you:

What the article says

That the right to travel can't be conditioned on a license.

What actually happened

The case was about a Virginia police chief's authority to issue permits. The court upheld the licensing requirement — it just said the right exists while being subject to reasonable regulation. The article uses the "right" language while omitting that the regulation survived.

Berberian v. Lussier (1958)

"The RIGHT to use an automobile on the public highways partakes of the nature of a liberty within the meaning of the Constitutional guarantees." Berberian v. Lussier, 139 A.2d 869 (R.I. 1958)
What the article says

That driving is a right, not a privilege.

What actually happened

Berberian was about license suspension procedures — whether the state must give you a hearing before taking your license away. The court said yes. It assumed a license was required. The holding was about due process, not whether licensing is constitutional.

This pattern repeats through the entire article. Every case quote is real. Every application is wrong.

The Full List of Mischaracterized Cases

People v. White (Cal. 1979)

DUE PROCESS CASE

The court held that police need reasonable suspicion before stopping a vehicle. That's a Fourth Amendment search-and-seizure case, not a licensing case. The quote about "fundamental constitutional right" is about freedom from arbitrary police stops — not freedom from licensing.

Adams v. City of Pocatello (Idaho 1966)

DUE PROCESS CASE

License revocation hearing. Same pattern as Berberian. The court said driving is a "right of liberty" — meaning you get due process before the state takes it away. Not that no license is required.

Shuttlesworth v. Birmingham (SCOTUS 1969)

FIRST AMENDMENT CASE — NOT VEHICLES

"Persons faced with an unconstitutional licensing law... may ignore the law." This is about parade permits and free speech. The Supreme Court held that an unconstitutional permit scheme for protest marches could be ignored. The case has zero to do with motor vehicles. No court has ever extended it to driver's licenses.

18 U.S.C. § 31 — The "Commercial" Definition

INAPPLICABLE

The article points out that federal law defines "motor vehicle" as "used for commercial purposes." This is accurate — for the federal criminal code. But state vehicle codes govern driver's licensing, and they define "motor vehicle" far more broadly. A federal criminal statute doesn't override state licensing law.

Wingfield v. Fielder (Cal. 1972)

PROFESSIONAL LICENSING — NOT DRIVING

The article claims this case says "no license is required when traveling without compensation." The actual case was about whether a state could require a license to practice a profession. The "traveling" language was about a person's right to move about freely — not about operating a motor vehicle.

The Rest: Equal Access Cases

The remainder of the article's citations — Campbell v. Walker, Simeone v. Lindsay, House v. Cramer, Brinkman v. Pacholike, Indiana Springs Co. v. Brown, Schlesinger v. City of Atlanta, Liebrecht v. Crandall, Chicago Motor Coach v. Chicago, Ex Parte Dickey, People v. Nothaus, Swift v. City of Topeka — are early 20th-century cases establishing that automobiles have equal rights to use highways as horses and wagons do.

That's all they say. They don't address licensing, and they predate modern traffic regulation entirely.

Why Hendrick v. Maryland (1915) Is the Only Case That Matters

Hendrick is the one case the article doesn't cite. It's the only U.S. Supreme Court case that directly addressed the question of whether a state can require a driver's license.

"In the absence of national legislation covering the subject, a state may rightfully prescribe uniform regulations necessary for public safety and order in respect to the operation upon its highways of all motor vehicles — those moving in interstate commerce as well as others." Hendrick v. Maryland, 235 U.S. 610 (1915) — Unanimous

The Court found "no solid foundation" for the argument that the right to travel includes a right to drive without a license. This has never been overruled.

What This Means for You

The viral article isn't wrong about everything. The right to travel is real. The quotes are real. But the article presents them in a way that implies they support something they don't.

If you cite Thompson v. Smith in court, a judge who reads the full case will see you're using a quote to support the opposite of what the case held. That doesn't help your argument — it destroys your credibility.

The right to travel deserves a better defense than misquoted case law.
